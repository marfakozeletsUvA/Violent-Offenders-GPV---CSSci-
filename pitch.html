Violence Against Journalists

Predicting Impunity Through Global Power Networks

GPV: Violent Offenders gonna slay

Potential RQ

Does Global North military intervention destabilize countries in ways that increase violence against journalists and does the international community then treat that violence as "expected", leading to systematic impunity?

The Argument

1

Global North countries intervene militarily (US, UK, France, NATO)

2

Interventions destabilize governance, create power vacuums

3

Journalists face heightened violence from armed groups & weakened states

4

Violence framed as "conflict-related" → less international pressure

5

Result: Higher impunity in intervention-affected countries

Prediction Target Options

Option A: Impunity (Prosecution Y/N)

  • Clean binary target variable
  • Direct policy relevance (who gets justice?)
  • Postcolonial critique is obvious
  • May have missing data on case outcomes
  • Prosecution ≠ conviction

Option B: Violence Severity

  • More granular (harassment → death)
  • Likely more complete data
  • Can examine escalation patterns
  • Severity scale may be subjective
  • Less direct policy lever

Postcolonial Bias Audit (Phase 2)

Critical Questions for the "Mindless Machine"

If network position predicts impunity better than crime severity...

This reveals how global power structures determine whose violence "matters"—the algorithm learns that geography is destiny.

If intervention-affected countries have systematically higher impunity...

The model reproduces a colonial logic: violence in "unstable" regions is expected, normalized, and therefore not actionable.

Who created the instability?

If Global North interventions destabilize, then high impunity in those countries is partly a consequence of intervener actions—but the model treats it as a country-level "feature."

Should a "fair" algorithm adjust for this?

Mitigation question: weight incidents equally regardless of network position? Or does that erase real differences in accountability structures?

Known Challenges

What We'll Need to Navigate

  • Military presence is complex—ranges from invasion to covert ops to arms sales
  • Covert destabilization is unmeasured (becomes part of postcolonial critique)
  • Core dataset scope unclear until we see CSV (Europe-only vs. global)
  • Causality is thorny—correlation between intervention and impunity ≠ causation
  • Network construction choices are consequential and must be justified
  • Sample size in intervention-affected countries may be small
